JOURNALISM

Journalism after 12th is a popular choice for students interested in media and communication. Here's a breakdown:

  • Eligibility Criteria: Typically, to pursue journalism after 12th, you need to have completed your high school education (12th grade) with a passing grade. Some journalism programs may also have specific subject requirements or minimum grades.
  • Duration: The duration of undergraduate journalism programs is usually three to four years, depending on the country and institution.
  • Career Prospects: A degree in journalism can lead to various career paths such as:
    1. Print journalism (newspapers, magazines)
    2. Broadcast journalism (television, radio)
    3. Online journalism (websites, blogs)
    4. Investigative journalism
    5. Photojournalism
    6. Public relations
    7. Corporate communications
    8. Freelance writing, editing, or reporting
  • Salary: Salaries in journalism can vary widely depending on factors such as location, experience, employer, and specialization. Entry-level positions may have lower salaries, but with experience, salaries can increase significantly.
  • Entrance Exams: Some journalism programs may require you to pass an entrance exam or meet specific admission requirements. These exams could test your aptitude in subjects like general knowledge, language skills, and current affairs.
  • Course Fees: The course fees for journalism programs can vary greatly depending on the institution, country, and whether it's a public or private university. It's essential to research and compare different programs to find one that fits your budget and offers quality education.
Overall, journalism can be a rewarding career for those passionate about storytelling, communication, and staying informed about current events. It offers opportunities for creativity, critical thinking, and making a difference through the power of media.
